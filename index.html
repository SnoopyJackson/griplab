LAPEL

The Lapel System is a Gi-exclusive entanglement meta that weaponizes the opponent's own uniform. The central mechanism: grab the bottom of their lapel, thread it through or around your leg, and create a structural bind that collapses their base in every direction simultaneously.

Unlike guard systems built on strength and timing, lapel systems win through structure. The Worm Guard wraps the lapel under the DLR-hooked leg, creating a lock so tight that passing requires fighting cloth — by which time your hips are already inverting for the back-take. The system chain: DLR → lapel grab → thread → Worm/Gubber → sweep or back-take → finish. At elite level (Keenan, Miyao brothers), the lapel is grabbed preemptively before any guard is established, making the whole game flow from cloth control outward.

SPDR
Bilateral sleeve control.

Spider/Lasso is a grip-and-frame meta built on the principle that two feet on two biceps creates more structural force than any passer can overcome. The opponent must break your frames to pass — and every frame-break opens a different attack. The system is inherently reactive and tree-like: it doesn't demand pre-planned sequences, but reads what the opponent does and punishes it instantly.

The Lasso variant is the system's evolution — threading one leg inside the arm creates a rotational anchor that frees the opposite hip for berimbolo, triangle, and DLR transitions. At advanced level, Spider and Lasso are not two guards but one system with two modes: Spider for extension/posture-breaking, Lasso for rotation/back-taking. Champions like Leandro Lo added DLR and lapel seamlessly, making the full system essentially unpassable at pace.

Core System Idea
DLR
Hook the lead leg.
Rotate around their axis.
The back is always open.
The DLR/Berimbolo system is a rotation-based back-taking meta. The outside leg hooks behind the opponent's lead leg, disrupting their lateral base and making linear passing impossible. The genius is geometric: their lead leg becomes a pivot point, and you rotate around it like a satellite — always moving toward where their back is exposed.

Berimbolo is the system's crown jewel: a controlled inversion using the DLR hook as a rotation axis, allowing the bottom player to appear behind the opponent without a dangerous scramble. At the Miyao level, the system becomes an infinite loop — missed berimbolo → reverse DLR → kiss of dragon → berimbolo again — never exposing back. The entire meta makes back-takes statistically inevitable. Combined with lapel grips, this is arguably the most dominant Gi bottom meta of the modern competition era.

The Collar-Sleeve System is the most universal and versatile bottom meta in Gi BJJ. It is the foundational grip configuration from which Spider, DLR, and lapel systems all branch. One hand on the deep collar (choke + posture control), one hand on the sleeve (arm control + direction-setting) — giving the bottom player a two-axis lever over the entire body.

What makes it a full meta-system is the sit-up game: from collar-sleeve, a practiced grappler sits up into their opponent and converts directly into single-leg attacks, arm drags, body locks, or guard-to-sweep chains. Bernardo Faria built 5 World Championships almost entirely on this concept. Combined with leg hooks (Spider + DLR), the collar-sleeve grip base becomes the architectural spine of every top-level Gi bottom game — it is not one system but the foundation layer all other systems build on.

Modern Closed Guard is a posture-destruction and submission-chain meta. The structure gives total control: legs locked around the waist remove hip mobility, and from inside the close, the bottom player controls both upper body (grips) and lower body (leg lock). The entire system activates from one catalyst: breaking posture.

Once the head is pulled down, a complete submission tree opens — cross-collar choke (Roger Gracie's highest-level weapon), kimura (chains to arm bar and hip bump sweep), triangle (built by hip elevation and sleeve control), omoplata. The "modern" element is the integration of high-guard mechanics, systematic posture-break drilling, and seamless transitions to open guard (Spider/DLR) when the closed guard is opened — treating guard-break as a trigger rather than a failure. Every posture-recovery attempt is fuel for a new attack.

Core System Idea
50/50
Equal entanglement.
Win the inside fight.

The 50/50 System is a mutual leg-entanglement meta that appears symmetric but rewards the player who understands inside-position mechanics. Both players have one leg inside the other's — but inside control on the entangled limb gives rotation advantage, leg lock access, and back-take priority.

In the Gi context specifically, 50/50 transforms dramatically: cloth grips on the inside leg prevent the mirror rotation that creates no-gi stalemates. A lapel thread around the entangled leg (Keenan's contribution) creates a one-way gate — you can rotate, they cannot. The system's goal is not to hold 50/50 but to pass through it: enter from DLR or berimbolo, establish inside control, prevent rotation with Gi grips, complete the back-take. Submission threats (ankle lock, kneebar, calf slicer) exist to force defensive straightening of the leg — which is exactly the opening that enables the back rotation.
